Analysis of my UI and UX.

A portfolio site is just there to provide the user with information. Therefore, the ability to traverse the site and finding the information easily is what I want my site to provide. To do this, the navigation will have to be well thought out and the UI needs to be neat and simple as not to confuse the user.

I started by designing my UX as Peta suggested in her notes. First, I thought about the information I was providing. It can be divided into three categories: Blog posts, Design information, and information about me. I believe that a user entering the portfolio site is either an employer, in which case they would want to see the information about me (my bio, projects I’ve worked on, my interests) or they are marking. If they are marking, they might be more interested in my blog posts. By dividing this information into sections, I save the user from having to swim through things they never wanted to look at so they can get to what they want. I also offer the user fewer choices to make at once, thus reducing the time it takes for the user to decide (Hick’s Law). The user first chooses between the three sections (this is the first decision). Then they choose between one of the pieces of information found within the sections. There is a maximum of 6 of these pieces of information. Had I not divided the information, the user would have to choose between 12 different pieces of information at one time.

Once the user selects a piece of information, they are taken to a screen where they will first find the article they have chosen in the centre of the screen. At the bottom of the screen, the user sees a collection of large buttons that they can use to navigate through information of the same section. I decided that they should be at the bottom of the page because this is where the user will end up when they are done reading through the information. This presented the problem of overflow. If I wanted to have the information in a container large enough for the user to be comfortable reading, the buttons would be hidden at the bottom of the page. To solve this, I increased their size and reduced the padding around them so that they would be closer to where the user stops reading (Fitts’ Law). This way, the buttons are visible, and the user will know that there’s more to the page if they scroll beyond the container.

The nav bar is there for people who are looking for “quick info”. If you’re here to read my blog posts or maybe to read about how I designed the web page, you probably clicked on the site with the intention of sticking around for a bit. Now let’s say you clicked on the site because you want to find my email address, or maybe my CV, or something like that. That kind of information, that you would simply check and then leave the site, is what I call “quick info”. This is what the buttons on the nav bar provide the user with.

Now we get to the UI.

I have given all the clickable elements on the site a border effect for when the user hovers over them. This just lets the user know that they can indeed click on the element they are looking at.

Since a large majority of the information given to the user is going to be text based, I want them to be comfortable reading for a long time. This is the reason for using relatively dull colours (black, white and grey). These colours together are very easy on the eyes, just like a newspaper. I’ve never seen somebody rub their eyes after reading one of those for a long time.

I have used a font from the Sans-Serif font family. Peta mentioned in her notes that they are good for readability and after some investigation of my own, I found that it was the best font family for an almost entirely text-based site. It has great legibility and is very comfortable to read.

My website is not overly complicated and has very few UI/UX elements to speak about. I believe that the few elements that are there, have come together to create an easy, comfortable browsing and reading experience. It may not be visually spectacular, but your eyes will thank you for that.
