Critical Reflection

For this exam I am required to submit a deployed portfolio website with good UI/UX and appropriate design and typography. It is also required to run on code with zero plagiarism. In this critical reflection I will be going over the sight and determining to what extent these requirements have been met.

UI/UX

My home page user interface allows the user to initially navigate to anything and everything they may need in one click. I have achieved this through what I like to call an “Open-Plan” home page. An open plan room is one in which there are little to no walls dividing it up into smaller sections. In a similar vein, on my home page, there is nothing keeping the user from any part of the website. The user can access all blog posts and pieces of information on that single page. I believe this is great because the user does not have to complete many actions to find what they came for. The drawback to this layout, however, is that it potentially increases the amount of time it takes the user to make a decision. Hick’s law states that “The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices.” My home page gives the user a lot of choices to make at once, which means that if they did not come to the sight with a specific piece of information in mind it may take them longer to decide what they would like to see first. I am willing to tolerate this drawback because I believe it is frustrating to have to wade through a series of lists and options. The user may take a moment longer to decide where they want to go, but once they do decide they will have a relatively easy time getting there.

On my content pages we see a scroll-bar system like the one on the home screen. This list of items contains articles that are similar or contain the same type of information as the information the user is currently looking at. So, for example, if you are looking at a blog post the scrollable items at the bottom of the page are a collection of other blog posts. This was inspired by the many social media platforms have algorithms that will show you more of what you like. Since I cannot create an algorithm to track the user and then alter the site to show them more of what they like I have decided to try and make that decision before hand by just grouping similar bits of information together. This has the effect of again reducing the number of actions the user must take to find information they would likely want to see. The drawback with this idea is that the user is not encouraged to explore the rest of the site once they are in a specific section. They may decide that they are done reading through blogs and then discover that there is much more that they could have seen. Or they could simply leave without seeing the other things. The only thing I’m hoping will prevent this from happening is the “About Me” section which is now on top (as suggested in the feedback I received). It is a short section and will definitely give people time to see that there is more to the site.

I have decided not to include a nav bar. From the feedback from assignment 2 I saw that my nav bar included a lot of unnecessary elements and adding the sections I already have to the nave bar would be redundant and require me to create new menu pages. This seemed to me like it would be a waste of effort since my home page acts as a kind of nav bar on its own.

The fonts used on my sight are all from the San Serif family. This family is known for having good readability. My colour scheme now includes some red and black in the background (inspired by the Anime.js website) to add some much-needed colour. These colours stay still on the home page so that all the fonts are always readable but move around on pages that have still white backgrounds. My creative additions have not significantly compromised the websites readability. I was initially worried about the moving background colours distracting the user from the content they need to read but after slowing down the animation and giving the website to a few people to look at I was able to conclude that the content is still readable with the moving background.
