Design Changes

My first website had the goal of being very simple, with minimal navigation methods and a colour scheme that was both clean and easy on the eye.

This site has similar goals, but with the BMW website as inspiration. My goal is still to keep the number of different navigation methods to a minimum. I have decided to have my navigation bar on the top of the page like normal websites. This is not only something that is familiar to most experienced web surfers, but it also gives me more screen space to work with and makes the technical work a little easier. Side navigation bars can be finicky when you’re tackling them for the first time. This decision was also inspired by the BMW website, which has a navigation bar on the top of the screen.

I also decided to have my home page dedicated to navigation. It should act as a sort of “hub” where the user can go to find a path to everything on the site in once place. This decision came with a problem. I want to have a clean, neat sight, but if I’m going to have everything on one page it might mess up that vision. My solution to this problem came to me when I was on the BMW website, browsing and looking at options for cars I can’t afford, and I noticed that their “Build & Price” pages hold a lot of different options and information without overloading the page. This is because some of the options they want you to chose from are hidden and scrollable, so the user only sees about 2 or 3 at a time. This is where I got the idea for “scrollable buttons” (I’m not sure what to call that section where you can scroll through a horizontal set of buttons) came from. The user can find and choose which ever piece of information they want without being overwhelmed by all the buttons and links on their screen.

The home page is separated into 3 groups of buttons (separated by headings): Blog Posts, Design Journey, and About me. I just thought it would be a nice, neat way to divide the information up. This is necessary because if I have millions of items you can scroll through, it will become extremely difficult for anybody to find anything. Right now, there are a maximum of 6 buttons in each section and I believe I can go up to 10 before the system will need to be changed.

Now you might be thinking “If these scrollable buttons exist, what’s the point of the nav bar?”. Good question. The nav bar is there for people who are looking for “quick info”. If you’re here to read my blog posts or maybe to read about how I designed the web page, you probably clicked on the site with the intention of sticking around for a bit. Now let’s say you clicked on the site because you want to find my email address, or maybe my CV, or something like that. That kind of information, that you would simply check and then leave the site, is what I call “quick info”. This is what the buttons on the nav bar provide the user with. An easy way to access information without having to liaise with my navigation system.

If you’re here for the juicy stuff, however, you’ll find that each page has a replica of the scrollable button system at the bottom. This is so that the user can quickly and easily switch between pieces of information under the same category as the one they are currently reading. This way, they don’t have to return to the home page to navigate unless they want to enter a different category of information.

The feedback from my previous submission mentioned something about my paragraphs starting and ending too close to the page. This made the text on the website difficult or awkward to read. To fix this, I put the text in a box and justified it. Now the text is at least 70mm (I measured) from the ends of the screen.

I used the same Helvetica font from the previous submission because it is simple and easy to read.
